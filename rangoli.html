some important lines for Rangoli competition

The people of India have kept the art of rangoli alive. The tradition is passed down in families, thus maintaining continuity. No brush or tool of any kind intervenes between the hand of the artist and the ground. This direct contact of the finger, powder or paste and ground allows the direct translation from her inner vision and experience into visible form. The designs drawn range from geometrical diagrams to curvilinear patterns or those inspired by nature.

India is a big country and land of many languages, and hence, this rangoli art form has different names in different regions. It is known as Sathiya in Gujarat, Kolam in Tamilnadu, Muggulu in Andhra Pradesh, Rangavali in Karnataka, Chowkpurana in some parts of Northern India, Mandana in Rajasthan, Aripana in Bihar, gion and so on. In fact, the term rangoli, as the art is known in Maharashtra, is a synonym for any kind of Indian floor art.

The word ‘rangoli’ is a derivation of the Sanskrit word ‘rangavalli, and it is comprised of two words, ‘rang’ meaning ‘colour’ and ‘aavalli’ meaning ‘coloured creepers’ or ‘row of colours’. And it signifies a creative expression of art by means of colours. Rangoli is also considered a lucky omen. Its popularity has remained unaffected over the years.

Rangoli is one of the most beautiful and pleasing art forms in India. It is a part of floor decoration and an expression of the creative self, often viewed as a form of self-portraiture. Rangoli is a traditional form of welcoming good luck. The emergence of this creative art can be attributed to some mythological literature works.
